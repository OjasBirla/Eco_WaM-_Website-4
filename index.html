What is Litter?

Storms on a beach cause tiny whirlwinds of waste blown about with the raging wind. Packaging waste jostles with aquatic creatures for space in the murky waters of the ocean.

When garbage is dumped randomly, it is called litter. Every year, just the amount of litter emptied into the oceans adds up to 9 billion tons.

Most of the litter comes from construction, demolishment, and mining. Municipal waste only adds to the burden.

Although littering is a thoughtless action, it is quite expensive to wash the footprints off. This is no issue that can be ignored. Waste management is wrought with hassles.

How Waste Management works

The age-old mantra we must acknowledge is 'Reduce, Reuse, Recycle' Reducing is about minimalism that separates need from greed and shirks obsessive consumerism.

Reusing is about creativity that envisages differently purposed products from the old ones that have outlived their function.

Recycling is about the responsibility to ensure the disposal of useless products to designated factories that segregate them into recyclable and non-recyclable waste.

Disposal

Disposal is the last stage of waste management Proper adherence to the Rs reduces the load at this stage. There are 3 primary methods of disposal.

Composting, Landfilling and Incineration

Composting allows for the disposal of the biodegradable waste into a pit and degrading it into compost. It is completely natural and causes no harm. Landfilling, where most types of waste are dumped and left open for quite a long period of time, is a real stumbling block of a city. The toxic fumes and stench breed germs, causing massive healthcare concerns, especially in developing countries.

Incineration, which is largely for hazardous medical waste, is where the waste is burnt and landfilled. This process also causes the same risks as landfilling, and is the source of most potent carcinogens.
